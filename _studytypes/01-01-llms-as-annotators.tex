\studytypesubsection{LLMs as Annotators}
\label{sec:llms-as-annotators}

\studytypeparagraph{Description}
In qualitative data analysis, manually annotating (``coding'') NL text (e.g., software artifacts, interview transcripts, open-ended survey responses) is a time-consuming manual process~\cite{DBLP:journals/ase/BanoHZT24}. LLMs can augment human coding, suggest new codes, and label artifacts based on a pre-defined coding guide much faster than humans can. 
%(see Section \synthesis), or even automate the entire qualitative data analysis process. 
\added{The extent to which, or under what conditions, LLMs can perform these tasks \textit{effectively} is debatable. Indeed, measuring their effectiveness is practically and philosophically challenging. From an interpretivist philosophical perspective, one cannot measure the quality of analysis by comparing one (human or machine) analyst's work to another. From a realist perspective, triangulating across multiple human judges, LLM judges, and other data sources (e.g., whether a pull request is marked as having resolved an issue) improves confidence in the findings but does not prove that any one judge is valid.}

\studytypeparagraph{Examples}
\citeauthor{Huang2023Enhancing}~\cite{Huang2023Enhancing} utilized multiple LLMs for joint annotation of mobile application reviews. 
They used three models of comparable size with an absolute majority voting rule (i.e., a label is only accepted if it receives more than half of the total votes from the models). This approach slightly outperformed the best individual model tested. 
Meanwhile, \citeauthor{DBLP:conf/msr/AhmedDTP25}~\cite{DBLP:conf/msr/AhmedDTP25} examined LLMs as annotators in SE research across five datasets, six LLMs, and ten annotation tasks.
They found that model-model agreement strongly correlates with human-model agreement; models performed poorly in tasks where humans also frequently disagreed. They proposed to use model confidence scores to identify specific samples that could be safely delegated to LLMs, potentially reducing human annotation effort without compromising inter-rater agreement.

\studytypeparagraph{Advantages}
LLMs are far faster and less expensive than human annotators, with studies showing cost reductions of 50-96\% on various NL tasks~\cite{DBLP:conf/emnlp/WangLXZZ21}.
For example, \citeauthor{DBLP:conf/chi/HeHDRH24} found that GPT-4 annotation required only two days and 122.08 USD compared to several weeks and 4,508 USD for a comparable MTurk pipeline~\cite{DBLP:conf/chi/HeHDRH24}.
Another study found that ChatGPT's accuracy exceeded crowd workers by approximately 25\%~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2303-15056}, and LLM's can achieve higher inter-rater agreement than crowd workers and trained annotators~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2303-15056}.

\studytypeparagraph{Challenges}
\added{In a recent open letter, 419 ``experienced qualitative researchers from 32 countries'' argued ``that analytic approaches such as reflexive thematic analysis are human research practices requiring a subjective, positioned, and reflexive researcher and therefore the use of GenAI in such approaches is not methodologically congruent'' or consistent with ``social and environmental justice''~\cite{jowsey2025reject}. In other words, using LLMs for qualitative data analysis entails serious philosophical and methodological problems.}

\reviewText{Many SE researchers appear to assume that individual annotations can be classified into ``correct'' vs. ``incorrect'' (e.g., that a particular software behavior is or is not a bug). The vast majority of qualitative researchers across the social sciences---both interpretivists and realists---do not think this way. They would say that whether a behavior is a bug depends on who you ask, and in what context; that is, sociotechnical phenomena are (at least partially) social constructed and inherently subjective.} 

\added{Even if we constrain our concern to code annotations with high theoretical consensus and limited value conflict,} LLMs have shown significant variability in annotation quality depending on the dataset and the annotation task~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2306-00176}. They are overconfident~\cite{DBLP:conf/kdd/WanSJKCNSSWYABJ24}. They exhibit consistent biases in label assignment, including tendencies to overestimate certain labels and misclassify neutral content~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2304-10145}. Context-dependent annotations pose a specific challenge, as LLMs show difficulty in correctly interpreting text segments that require broader contextual understanding~\cite{DBLP:conf/chi/HeHDRH24}. When their annotations are incorrect, LLMs can negatively affect human judgment~\cite{DBLP:conf/www/HuangKA23a}. 

Furthermore, \added{LLMs can be unreliable for high-stakes labeling with substantial downstream consequences (e.g., safety-critical or compliance-related decisions)~\cite{DBLP:conf/chi/Wang0RMM24}. They can exhibit uneven per-class performance, with accuracy differing markedly across label categories, often performing worse on rare or ambiguous labels and systematically over- or under-assigning specific annotation categories~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2304-10145}.} Their consistency often falls below suggested reliability thresholds, with outputs being sensitive to minor prompt variations~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2304-11085}. Pooling multiple outputs and multiple runs of the same input to enable majority voting improves reliability but increases cost and carbon footprint~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2304-11085}. LLM annotation requires careful management of per token charges, particularly for longer texts~\cite{DBLP:conf/emnlp/WangLXZZ21}. Cost comparison between LLM-based and human annotation thus remains controversial~\cite{DBLP:conf/chi/HeHDRH24}.

In summary, LLMs may be better for augmenting rather than replacing human annotators~\cite{DBLP:conf/emnlp/WangLXZZ21, DBLP:conf/chi/HeHDRH24}, and even then, there is limited compelling evidence that such augmentation increases \textit{effectiveness} as well as efficiency.
